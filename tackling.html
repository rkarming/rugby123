Tackling

It is very important to learn how to tackle correctly in rugby because demonstrating proper tackling technique can help reduce the risk of injury. First and foremost, all tackles must be made below the shoulders. Tackling another player above the shoulders is considered dangerous play (a high tackle) and the opposing team will be awarded a penalty kick.

There are several types of tackles, but one of the most commonly used is the side tackle. To prepare for this type of tackle, stay on the balls of your feet (the part of the foot between the arch and toes) and bend your knees. Keep your chin up and your eyes forward, and square your hips and chest. Approach your opponent from either side and, while focusing on his/her thigh, use your legs to launch yourself at your opponent. Contact your opponent's thigh with your inside shoulder. More specifically, if you are approaching your opponent from his/her right side, contact his/her right thigh with your right shoulder. If you are approaching your opponent from his/her left side, contact his/her left thigh with your left shoulder. Make sure you keep your head behind your opponent (opposite from the shoulder that you are tackling with) and wrap your arms around his/her legs. After the tackle is made, make sure that you get back up on your feet before you touch the ball. Otherwise, this will result in a penalty.

Another common type of tackle is the dive tackle. This is similar to the side tackle, except you approach your opponent from behind and focus on both of his/her thighs. When you are close enough to your opponent, use your legs to launch yourself at him/her, keep your head to either side of him/her (opposite from the shoulder that you are tackling with), and wrap your arms around his/her legs.

If you are trailing behind your opponent and you cannot get close enough to wrap your arms around him/her, you can use an ankle tap tackle. This is when you use your legs to launch yourself at your opponent and, while focusing on his/her feet, use your arm or hand to tap one of his/her feet. This is usually enough to cause a player running at full speed to stumble and fall.

Timing and commitment are key to performing a good tackle. You must time your launch while running so that you contact your opponent in the correct spot. If you time your launch incorrectly, you may end up launching yourself into your opponent's boot (rugby shoe) or missing your opponent completely. Furthermore, you must commit to your tackles. If you show any indication of indecisiveness, you may tackle your opponent incorrectly or miss your tackle.
