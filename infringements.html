Infringements

In rugby, an infringement is the breaking of a law. When an infringement is committed, the referee will blow the whistle and stop play, unless the non-offending team gains an advantage from the act. Depending on the severity of the infringement, play is restarted either with a scrum, penalty kick, or free kick (see Gameplay for more on restarting play).

Here are some of the most common infringements:

Forward Throw
A player cannot throw or pass the ball forward (i.e. toward the opposition's dead ball line). If the forward throw is unintentional, then a scrum will be awarded. However, if the forward throw is intentional, then a penalty kick will be awarded to the non-offending team. Note: it is not considered a forward throw if the ball hits a player or the ground and it bounces forward.
Knock-on
A knock-on occurs when a player loses possession of the ball and it goes forward, touching the ground or another player before the original player can catch it. If the knock-on is unintentional, then a scrum will be awarded. However, if the knock-on is intentional, then a penalty kick will be awarded to the non-offending team. Note: it is not considered a knock-on if a player rips or forcefully knocks the ball from the ball carrier's hands and the ball goes forward. It is also not considered a knock-on if a player charges down or blocks the ball immediately after it is kicked and the ball goes forward.
Obstruction
This occurs when a player intentionally moves or stands in a position that blocks an opponent from tackling the ball carrier. A penalty kick will be awarded for obstruction.
Offside
In rugby, the ball acts as the (constantly moving) offside line. Thus, in general, a player is offside if s/he is in front of a teammate who is carrying the ball. A player can only be penalized for being offside if s/he interferes with play or moves forward toward the ball while in an offside position. When a player is penalized for being offside, the non-offending team chooses either a penalty kick at the place of infringement or a scrum at the place where the offending team last played the ball.
Not Releasing the Tackled Player
After a tackle is made, the tackler must immediately release his/her opponent. Failing to release the tackled player results in a penalty kick.
Not Releasing the Ball
After a tackle is made, the tackled player must immediately release the ball. The tackled player must get up before playing the ball. Failing to release the ball results in a penalty kick.
Incorrect Throw-In
During a lineout and a scrum, the ball must be thrown in straight and without delay. An incorrect throw-in results in a free kick.
Unfair Play
Unfair play consists of the following:
Intentionally offending
A player cannot intentionally commit infringements or play unfairly. This results in a penalty kick.
Time wasting
A player cannot intentionally waste time. This results in a free kick.
Throwing into touch
A player cannot intentionally knock, place, push, or throw the ball over the touchline, touch-in-goal line, or dead ball line. This results in a penalty kick.
Dangerous Play and Misconduct
If any of the following occur, the non-offending team is awarded a penalty kick:
Punching, kicking, or striking
A player cannot punch, kick, or strike an opponent with his/her fist, hand, arm, elbow, shoulder, head, knee, or foot.
Stamping or trampling
A player cannot stamp or trample on an opponent.
Tripping
A player cannot trip an opponent with his/her leg or foot.
Dangerous tackling
A player cannot tackle an opponent early, late, or above the shoulder line. Furthermore, a player cannot tackle an opponent whose feet are off the ground.
Playing an opponent without the ball
A player who is not in possession of the ball cannot hold, push, or obstruct an opponent not carrying the ball, except in a scrum, ruck, or maul.
Dangerous charging
Charging includes making contact with another player without using the arms, or without grasping a player. A player cannot charge or knock down an opponent who is carrying the ball. Additionally, a player cannot charge into a ruck or maul.
Late-charging the kicker
A player cannot intentionally charge or obstruct an opponent who has just kicked the ball.
Tackling a jumper in the air
A player cannot tackle or tap, nor push or pull the foot of an opponent who is jumping for the ball.
Lifting a player from the ground and driving or dropping him/her into the ground
A player cannot lift an opponent into the air and drive or drop him/her into the ground such that his/her upper body or head come into contact with the ground.
Dangerous play in a scrum, ruck, or maul
A player in the front row of a scrum cannot rush against an opponent, intentionally lift an opponent off of his/her feet, or force an opponent upwards out of a scrum. A player cannot intentionally collapse a ruck, maul, or scrum. Furthermore, a player cannot charge into a ruck or maul without binding onto a player already involved in the ruck or maul.
Retaliation
A player cannot retaliate against an opponent.
Misconduct
A player cannot commit acts contrary to good sportsmanship. A player cannot commit any misconduct while the ball is out of play.

Yellow and Red Cards

In rugby, a yellow card is issued for committing offenses such as obstruction, unfair play, dangerous play and misconduct, or repeated infringements. A player who receives a yellow card is suspended from the game for 10 minutes. This is commonly referred to as being sent to the "sin bin." The player's team must play with one less player until the 10 minutes are up.

A red card is issued for committing the same types of offenses as with yellow cards. Red cards are usually issued when the offense is more serious. Additionally, if a player receives a second yellow card during a game, s/he will automatically be issued a red card. A red card indicates that a player must be sent off and s/he can no longer participate in the match. The player's team must finish the game with one less player.
