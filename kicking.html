Kicking

There are various types of kicks used in rugby depending on the goal to be achieved and the situation. Here are some of the most common types:

Drop Kick

The drop kick is used to kick off games and restart games after a team scores. It is also an option used to restart play after an infringement is called (see Restarting Play: Penalty Kicks/Free Kicks on the Gameplay page). Additionally, the drop kick can be used to score a conversion or drop goal (see Scoring on the Gameplay page).

To perform a drop kick, drop the ball vertically so that one of its ends comes into contact with the ground. Put your weight on your non-kicking foot and as the ball bounces up off of the ground, bring your kicking foot forward to make contact with the bottom of the ball. Make sure to follow through your kick (i.e. do not stop your kick right after making contact with the ball).

Place Kick

The place kick is an option used to restart play after an infringement is called (see Restarting Play: Penalty Kicks/Free Kicks on the Gameplay page). Additionally, the place kick can be used to score a conversion (see Scoring on the Gameplay page).

To perform a place kick, place the ball diagonally on the ground (or on the kicking tee) so that the top end of the ball is facing away from you. Measure the distance you need to run up to the ball. When you run up to the ball, plant your non-kicking foot slightly behind the ball and strike the ball with the laces on your kicking foot. Make sure to follow through your kick (i.e. do not stop your kick right after making contact with the ball).

Punt

The punt is an option used to restart play after an infringement is called (see Restarting Play: Penalty Kicks/Free Kicks on the Gameplay page). It can also be used to gain ground during play.

To perform a punt, hold the ball vertically out in front of you with one end of the ball facing the sky and the other end of the ball facing the ground. Drop the ball and strike it with the laces on your kicking foot before it touches the ground. Make sure to follow through your kick (i.e. do not stop your kick right after making contact with the ball).

Quick Tap

The quick tap (aka tap and go) is an option used to restart play after an infringement is called (see Restarting Play: Penalty Kicks/Free Kicks on the Gameplay page). The quick tap is best used when the opposition appears vulnerable to an immediate attack. For example, in the clip below, Argentina (blue and white) restarts play, after a penalty, with a quick tap and catches the opposition off guard.

To perform a quick tap, the ball must leave your hands and make contact with any part of your leg below the knee, except your heel. This type of kick is essentially like performing a mini punt to yourself.

Grubber Kick

The grubber kick is a low kick used to get the ball behind the opposition. For example, the grubber kick could be used in the following situation: a player is close to the opposition's goal line, but s/he is unable to easily carry the ball past his/her opponents or pass to his/her teammates. The player could use a grubber kick to roll the ball past his/her opponents and into the in-goal area.

To perform a grubber kick, hold the ball vertically out in front of you with one end of the ball facing the sky and the other end of the ball facing the ground. Drop the ball onto the laces of your kicking foot and make contact with the end of the ball closest to you.

Up-and-Under

The up-and-under kick is used to penetrate the opposition's defense to gain ground. For example, the up-and-under kick could be used in the following situation: a player catches a high ball at his/her 22 meter line, has no passing options, and is confronted by many opposing players who are chasing after the ball. With this type of kick, the ball goes high in the air, giving the kicking team's players time to run under the ball before it returns to the ground.

To perform an up-and-under, hold the ball vertically out in front of you with one end of the ball facing the sky and the other end of the ball facing the ground. Putting your weight on your non-kicking foot, drop the ball and bring your kicking foot forward to make contact with the bottom of the ball. Kick the ball with the laces of your shoe, closer to your toes.

Chip and Chase

The chip and chase (aka chip kick) resembles the up-and-under. However, whereas the up-and-under is used to penetrate the opposition's defensive line, the chip and chase is a shorter kick used to move the ball over an opponent's head and into the empty space behind him/her. Remember that players cannot tackle an opponent unless s/he is carrying the ball.

To perform a chip and chase, hold the ball vertically out in front of you with one end of the ball facing the sky and the other end of the ball facing the ground. Putting your weight on your non-kicking foot, drop the ball and bring your kicking foot forward to make contact with the bottom of the ball. Kick the ball with the laces of your shoe, but do not put as much force behind your kick as the up-and-under. Once you kick the ball, immediately run after it. Ideally, you will catch the ball right behind the opponent that you are trying to beat.
