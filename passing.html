Passing

Before you learn how to pass the ball, you must learn how to hold it. First, hold the ball out horizontally in front of you with both hands in the middle on either side of the ball. If you are right-handed, slide your right hand back (toward your body) a few inches so that it is near the end of the ball. Then, slide your left hand forward (away from your body) a few inches so that it is near the tip of the ball. Make sure to keep your fingers spread out so that you can get a good grip on the ball. Your right hand is used to throw the ball and your left hand merely acts as a guide. If you are left-handed, move your left hand back and your right hand forward. You will be throwing the ball with your left hand and guiding it with your right hand.

The most common type of pass used in rugby is the spin pass. However, as beginners, you should first learn how to throw the ball without spin. Remember that you must always pass the ball backwards! If you are right-handed, you will start by passing from right to left. Start with the ball at your right hip. As you begin moving it to the left side of your body, take a step forward with your left foot, turn your hips to the left, aim the tip of the ball at your target, and release the ball using the momentum from your rotating body. Make sure to follow through your pass and keep your hands aimed at your target after releasing the ball. You will do the opposite if you are left-handed.

Once you feel comfortable passing the ball without spin, you can move on to the spin pass. This type of pass uses the same concepts, but it adds spin to the ball to increase accuracy and distance. Remember that your non-dominant hand is just used to guide the ball, not to spin the ball! Thus, to start, try passing only using your dominant hand. If you are right-handed, hold the ball with your right hand and go through all the previously mentioned steps. However, just before releasing the ball, flick your wrist and fingers inward to add (counterclockwise) spin to the ball. The more power you add to your pass from your hips and triceps, the farther the ball will travel. Again, everything will be reversed if you are left-handed. When you are ready, add your non-dominant hand to better guide the ball to your target.

Once you feel comfortable passing with your dominant hand, try passing with your non-dominant hand. There will be times that you will need to pass with your non-dominant hand in games. Throwing a rugby ball looks easier than it is, but practice makes perfect!
