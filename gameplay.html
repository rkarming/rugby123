Gameplay

Starting the Game

Before each rugby game, the referee facilitates a coin toss between the team captains. One team's captain tosses the coin while the other calls "heads" or "tails" to decide which team wins the toss. The captain of the team that wins the toss chooses to either kick off or select an end of the field. If the winner chooses to kick off, then the opposing team gets to select an end. Likewise, if the winner chooses to select an end, then the opposing team must kick off.

Each rugby game is started with a kick off (drop kick) from the half way line. Either team may gain possession of the ball, as long as it travels beyond the receiving team's 10 meter line.

Moving the Ball

In rugby, every player has the option of running with the ball, passing it, or kicking it. However, since the ball cannot be passed forward (only laterally or backward), players must either run with it or kick it to gain ground. In the game of rugby, play is continuous and, in general, time is not stopped unless a player is injured.

Running

The purpose of running the ball is to gain ground by running toward the opponent's goal line. Any player may run with the ball until s/he is tackled, steps out of bounds, or runs past the opponent's goal line. In rugby, the ball acts as the (constantly moving) offside line so the ball carrier cannot run behind his/her own teammates. In other words, unlike in football, blocking is not permitted.

Passing

Players pass the ball laterally or backward to teammates in order to change the point of attack, as well as to make tackling more difficult for the opposing team. Since the ball is the offside line, the ball carrier cannot pass the ball forward, only laterally or backward.

Kicking

Players kick the ball to advance it or to relieve their team of poor field position. Any player can kick the ball forward at any time during a game. The ball can be retrieved by either team. However, the ball is the offside line so the kicker's teammates can only recover the ball if they are behind the kicker when the ball is kicked, or if the kicker (or someone behind the kicker) moves past them to put them on-side.

Tackling

In rugby, only the ball carrier may be tackled. Players tackle the ball carrier if s/he is on the opposing team in order to prevent him/her from gaining more ground. In general, tackles are made around the legs and waist, but tackles should be no higher than the shoulders. Time does not stop when a tackle is made.

Rucks

When the ball carrier is tackled, s/he must "place" the ball on the ground and roll away from it, if possible, so that play can continue. One or more players from each team make physical contact over the placed ball, attempting to drive the opposition back far enough to gain control of the ball. This is called a ruck. Players can only enter a ruck from behind, not from the sides. Additionally, players may not pick up or otherwise interfere with the ball until it is cleared from the ruck (i.e. until one team is driven back far enough to expose the ball). Rucking correctly is critical in rugby because it is a key way to gain or retain possession of the ball.

Mauls

Mauls are similar to rucks, except they are formed when the ball carrier is held up, not tackled to the ground. They consist of three or more players who are in physical contact, attempting to gain control of the ball. Like with rucks, players can only enter mauls from behind. A maul ends if the ball touches the ground or a player emerges with the ball.

Scoring

Points can be scored in the following ways:

Try (five points)
A team can score a try by grounding the ball, or touching the ball down, in the opposing team's in-goal area.
Penalty Try (five points)
If a defending team's misconduct prevents the attacking team from scoring a try and the referee believes that a try would have been scored, the referee may award the attacking team with a penalty try.
Conversion (two points)
After a try is scored, the scoring team may then attempt a conversion by drop kicking or place kicking the ball over the crossbar between the opposing team's goal posts. The kick can be taken from anywhere on the field of play as long as it is parallel to the touchlines, and it is in line with the point where the ball was grounded for the try. Thus, conversion kicks are made easier when a try is scored close to the center of the in-goal area. (See Kicking for more about drop kicks and place kicks.)
Drop Goal (three points)
A team can score a drop goal at any time during open play by drop kicking the ball over the crossbar between the opposing team's goal posts. (See Kicking for more about drop kicks.)
Penalty Goal (three points)
If a team commits a deliberate infringement, or breaks the rules, the referee may award the team that did not commit the infringement with a penalty kick. A team can score with a penalty kick by punting, drop kicking, or place kicking the ball over the crossbar between the opposing team's goal posts. (See Kicking for more about punts, drop kicks, and place kicks.)

Restarting Play

After scoring, the game is restarted with a restart kick. The scoring team takes a drop kick from the half way line. Either team may gain possession of the ball, as long as it travels beyond the receiving team's 10 meter line.

Furthermore, time only stops in rugby if the referee calls an infringement or if the ball goes out of bounds. In these cases, there are several methods to restart play.

Scrums

If the referee calls an infringement, play may be restarted with a scrum. This is where the pack of forwards binds together. More specifically, the hooker (#2) and two props (#1 and #3) bind together by wrapping their arms around each other. The hooker positions him/herself between the two props. This is called the front row. The two locks (#4 and #5) bind on behind the front row by getting down on one knee and putting their heads between the props and the hooker. This is called the second row. The number eight (#8) and two flankers (#6 and #7) bind on behind the second row. The number eight positions him/herself between the two flankers. This is called the back row.

The two packs (one from each team) position themselves within an arm's length of each other. The referee then issues three commands, in the following order: crouch, touch, and engage. When the referee says crouch, the opposing two front rows crouch. When the referee says touch, each team's props touch the outside shoulder of the opposing team's props. Finally, when the referee says engage, both front lines thrust forward and bind onto the opposing team's front line, forming a tunnel between the two front rows.

The non-offending team's scrumhalf rolls the ball down the center of the tunnel. Each team pushes forward until one front row player, usually the hooker, hooks the ball backward with his/her feet. The ball is then moved backward by the second and back rows. Finally, the scrumhalf (#9) retrieves the ball and puts it into play.

Penalty Kicks

If the referee calls an infringement, a penalty kick may be awarded to the non-offending team. The offending team must back up 10 yards so that the non-offending team can restart play unopposed. The player who takes the penalty kick can choose to punt, drop kick, place kick, or quick tap the ball to restart play (see Kicking for more information on the types of kicks).

A team can score a penalty goal from a penalty kick. The player who takes the penalty kick can decide whether to signal to the referee that s/he intends to kick for goal. If s/he signals to the referee, s/he can choose to punt, drop kick, or place kick the ball. Furthermore, the offending team's players must remain stationary with their hands resting by their sides until the kick is taken. On the other hand, if the player taking the kick does not signal to the referee that s/he intends to kick for goal, s/he can only score with a drop kick.

If the non-offending team is awarded a penalty kick and they are not within range to score, they can kick for touch. This means kicking the ball up the field and out of bounds (over the touchline) in order to gain field advantage. This results in a lineout, with the kicking team throwing in the ball.

Free Kicks

If the referee calls an infringement, a free kick may be awarded to the non-offending team. A free kick is taken immediately from the point at which the infringement occurred. The player who takes the free kick can choose to punt, drop kick, place kick, or quick tap the ball to restart play (see Kicking for more information on the types of kicks). Unlike with a penalty kick, a team cannot score from a free kick.

Lineouts

If the ball goes out of bounds, play is restarted with a lineout. In a lineout, both teams form a line perpendicular to the touchline, one meter apart from one another. Players may be supported in the air by their teammates. A player from the non-offending team (the team that did not last touch the ball before it went out of bounds) throws the ball in a straight line, directly between the two lines of teams. Each team attempts to gain possession of the ball.
