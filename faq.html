That depends.

Rugby, played correctly, is no more dangerous than any other contact sport. Like with other sports, injuries are more common among players who are not in good physical condition. Although rugby players do not wear helmets or pads, several studies have shown that ruggers experience fewer head and neck injuries than players of other sports like football. Ruggers are taught proper tackling techniques and forms to minimize injuries. Furthermore, when tackled, players' bodies collide with other bodies, not with hard plastic helmets or other gear. With that said, it is common to have bruises or minor cuts after playing rugby since pads are not used.

Rugby tradition encourages all players to “leave it on the field.”

In other words, regardless of what happens on the pitch during the match, ruggers shake hands and socialize once the game is over. The home team is expected to provide a post-game meal (and oftentimes, adult beverages for teams with players over 21) for the visiting team, as a sign of respect and appreciation for their travels. These post-game meals and entertainment are commonly referred to as socials. During rugby socials, players from both teams bond, discuss the game, sing rugby songs, and form friendships.

No, you can play rugby at any age.

There are youth and high school rugby teams, as well as club teams for adults. USA Rugby divides the youth and high school groups by grade level, not by age. For example, 1st and 2nd graders are permitted to play together, 3rd and 4th graders, 5th and 6th graders, etc.

That depends.

The cost to play rugby varies widely, depending on the team you play for, as well as the style and quality of the equipment you purchase. Here are some of the typical costs associated with joining and playing on a rugby team:

  • Club Dues. Players must pay club dues, which typically contribute to league entry fees, referees, and field maintenance. However, the cost of club dues ranges greatly. Some teams may also include the cost of training equipment, game jerseys, team warm-up clothes, travel, meals served at the post-game socials, etc. in the club dues amount. Furthermore, dues can vary depending on how competitive your team is, where you are located, and if your team receives any additional funding. For example, club dues for an individual playing for a university-supported club team that participates in many fundraising events could be as low as $50/season. On the other hand, club dues for an individual playing for a non-university women's team could be $350 for the first season to cover team warm-up gear (which could be used in following seasons).
  • USA Rugby Member Fee. Players participating in official matches must pay a fee to become a USA Rugby member (often referred to as getting CIPP'ed). This gives players USA Rugby accident insurance, which covers damage inflicted upon people or objects during official gameplay. For example, if a CIPP'ed player kicks a ball into a car during an official game and dents it, s/he will not be liable for the damage. Getting CIPP'ed costs approximately $45/year.
  • Equipment. Most teams require players to purchase their own equipment, which could include a mouth guard, shorts, socks, and rugby boots (soccer cleats are usually an acceptable alternative). This is approximately $120, on average.

Many places!

Rugby equipment can be purchased in-person in most sporting goods stores such as at Dick's Sporting Goods. It can also be purchased from a variety of stores online such as Amazon.com, WorldRugbyShop.com, RugbyImports.com, etc.
